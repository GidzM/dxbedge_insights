Dubai’s real estate market delivered record-breaking performance, with total transactions reaching 
AED 917 billion (USD 250 billion), representing a 20% year-on-year increase. Growth was driven by 
sustained demand for off-plan developments and ultra-luxury assets, while investors benefited from 
some of the world’s most attractive rental yields, averaging 8–10%. 
Momentum continued into 2025, with industry analysts pointing to a transition into a more stable 
and mature market cycle rather than a correction, despite a forecast increase in supply. Market 
resilience is reinforced by a high proportion of cash buyers, reducing leverage risk and supporting 
price stability. 
Importantly, Dubai’s real estate fundamentals are underpinned by long-term, government-backed 
growth strategies, including the Dubai 2040 Urban Master Plan and the D33 Economic Agenda, which 
prioritize population growth, infrastructure investment, and economic diversification. Few global 
markets offer this level of sovereign-led planning and commitment, positioning Dubai as a 
compelling long-term investment destination.

Buyer Protection (Important for Investors) 
Dubai has one of the most regulated off-plan frameworks globally: 
Escrow accounts: Buyer funds are legally ring-fenced and can only be released to the developer as

Milestone-based disbursements: Reduces completion risk 
This structure materially lowers counterparty risk compared to many emerging markets.

Mortgages in Dubai — Residents vs. Non-Residents

Residents (Expats & UAE Visa Holders) 
UAE residents (holding a residence visa) can apply for mortgages in Dubai. 
Banks generally prefer applicants with UAE income, Emirates ID, and UAE credit history. 
Typical minimum monthly income requirements start around AED 10,000–15,000+ (varies by lender

 Non-Residents (Foreign Investors Without UAE Residency) 
Foreign nationals can also obtain mortgages, even without a UAE visa. 
Eligibility depends on stable income, international credit history (not always required), and 
documentation from home country. 
Many lenders require proof of employment and substantial bank statements.

4% of the property’s purchase price (the biggest single cost).

Registration fee:

Oqood Fee (Off-Plan Only) 
A one-time fee for registering an off-plan sales contract with the DLD: approx. AED 1,000 + 5% VAT.  
 
Broker / Agent and Professional Fees 
Real Estate Agent Commission 
Standard pay is 2% of the purchase price + 5% VAT (over the commission). 
For off-plan buyers, the developer pays the agent’s commission.

licensed trustee office.  (does not apply to off plan purchases)

Property Valuation Fee 
Payable to the lender to appraise the property’s value: usually AED 2,500–3,500 + VAT.

In aggregate, total transaction fees for secondary market purchases typically run 7–8% of the

Investor Notes 
No annual property tax or capital gains tax: once you pay the buy-side fees, Dubai does not levy 
recurring property taxes as many Western countries do.  
Buyers often need cash for fees: many of these costs (especially the DLD fee and agent commission) 
can’t be financed through a mortgage and must be paid in cash at closing.  
 
Off-plan can be cheaper upfront: developers cover commission and sometimes part of the DLD fee to 
incentivize buyers, but review all terms carefully.  

Investor Takeaway 
When budgeting for a Dubai property purchase in the secondary market, expect about 7–8% of the 
purchase price in transaction costs beyond the price itself. These include mandatory Dubai Land 
Department fees (not negotiable), broker commissions, trustee/admin fees, and, if applicable, 
mortgage-related costs. Planning for these up front ensures you accurately assess total capital

Landlord/Investor-focused summary of the key RERA rules and obligations in Dubai’s rental market - 
framed around what property owners must comply with to stay legal and avoid disputes: 
Core RERA Rules for Landlords in Dubai 
Mandatory Tenancy Contract & Registration with Ejari 
All lease agreements must be in writing and registered with Ejari — RERA’s official tenancy system — 
for legal recognition. 
Without Ejari registration, the contract isn’t enforceable and neither party has protection under the 
law.  
 
Landlord Obligations Under Dubai Tenancy Law 
Property Condition & Handover 
Landlords must deliver the property in good, habitable condition, suitable for the intended use as per 
the contract. 
If the property is unfinished, parties must agree formally how it will be made suitable before use.  
 
Maintenance & Repairs 
Unless the contract specifies otherwise, landlords are responsible for major maintenance and repairs 
throughout the lease term — including defects not caused by tenant misuse. 
This generally includes structural repair, plumbing, electrical, and damage beyond normal wear and 
tear.  
Tenants are contractually responsible for minor maintenance usually up to a maximum of 1,000 AED. 
 
No Detrimental Alterations 
Landlords may not make changes that hinder the tenant’s full use of the property. Work must be 
agreed and, where required, approved by authorities.  
 
Rent Setting & Increases 
Rule-Based Rent Increase 
Landlords can only increase rent at renewal and based on the official RERA Rental Index — a 
calculator published by RERA/DLD that benchmarks rents by area and unit type. 
Increases outside the permitted index range can be challenged and rejected at the Rental Dispute 
Settlement Centre.  

Notice Requirements 
A 90-day written notice must be given before the end of the contract to propose rent changes or 
renewal terms.  
 
Eviction & Termination Rules 
Contractual Protection 
Landlords cannot terminate a contract unilaterally during its term. 
To lawfully ask a tenant to vacate on renewal, a formal eviction notice with a valid reason (e.g., 
owner’s personal use, sale, major refurbishment) must be served at least 12 months before expiry 
and through proper channels (notary or registered service).  
 
Security Deposit & Utilities 
While RERA doesn’t directly set a maximum deposit amount, landlords must return security deposits 
promptly at the end of the lease, minus justified deductions for damage beyond normal wear and 
tear.  
Landlords cannot disconnect utilities or lock tenants out without legal cause — such actions are 
prohibited and can result in penalties.  
 
Dispute Resolution 
Any landlord–tenant dispute (rent, eviction, deposit) is adjudicated through the Rental Dispute 
Settlement Centre (RDSC), a tribunal established under law to provide enforceable judgments.  
Practical Compliance Checklist for Landlords 
Before renting: 
Prepare a detailed, clear tenancy contract specifying rent, term, deposit, and obligations. 
Register the lease with Ejari (or confirm tenant has done so). 
Ensure the property is in compliant, habitable condition. 
During the lease: 
Maintain the property and respond promptly to repair obligations. 
Only propose rent increases within RERA index limits and with 90-day written notice. 
Respect tenant privacy and lawful use. 
 
At lease end/renewal: 
If intending non-renewal, serve a formal 12-month notice with a lawful ground. 

Handle deposits fairly and with documentation supporting any deductions. 
File necessary renewal contracts or updates with Ejari within required timelines.  
Tenants have the legal right to renew the tenancy contract.  
 
Investor Takeaway 
RERA’s framework in Dubai balances landlord rights with tenant protections. As a property owner, 
compliance means registering leases properly, maintaining the asset, adhering to rent increase and 
eviction regulations, and using formal dispute channels when needed. Following these rules not only 
avoids legal pitfalls but also enhances investor confidence and tenancy stability.  
 
Investor-oriented explanation of Dubai’s RERA Rental Index rules for rent increases and the legally 
valid eviction reasons landlords can cite under Dubai tenancy law: 
RERA Rent Increase Rules in Dubai 
Dubai’s rent increase regime is governed by RERA’s Rental Index Calculator and Decree No. (43) of 
2013. It limits rent increases at renewal based on how the current rent compares to average market 
rents for similar properties in the same area.

If the current rent is close to or above the market rate, a landlord may not raise the rent at all. Any 
increase above these limits is considered invalid and can be challenged before RERA’s Rental Dispute 
Settlement Centre (RDSC).  
 
Key Procedural Rules 
90 days’ notice before contract expiry is required for any rent change.  
Both parties can consult the RERA Rent Calculator (via Dubai Land Department) to confirm what 
increases are permitted.  
Tenants can refuse a rent increase that exceeds the calculable limit.  
Any contractual clause that tries to circumvent RERA’s lawful limits (e.g., automatic blanket 
percentage increases) is void.  
 
Valid Eviction Grounds for Landlords in Dubai 
Under Dubai’s Tenancy Law (Law No. 26 of 2007, as amended by Law No. 33 of 2008), landlords can 
only request tenants to vacate for specific legal reasons — arbitrary or rent-increase refusal is not a 
valid ground.  
 
Required 12-Month Notice Evictions 
Landlords must give a formal, notarized or registered 12-month notice before the lease ends for valid 
eviction reasons such as:  
Sale of the property; 
Use of the property for personal use or for a first-degree relative; 
Major renovation or refurbishment requiring vacancy; 
Demolition or redevelopment of the property.  
 
Immediate Eviction (During Tenancy) 
A landlord can seek eviction and payment orders through the RDSC without waiting for contract 
expiry if the tenant:  
Fails to pay rent within 30 days after written notice; 
Subleases without consent; 
 Uses the property for illegal, immoral, or unauthorized purposes; 

 Causes significant damage beyond normal wear and tear; 
 Violates zoning or permitted usage (e.g., illegal commercial use).  
 
Investor Takeaways 
Rent Increases Are Rule-Bound 
You cannot simply raise rent by market demand — only by what the RERA Index permits.  
Compliance with the 90-day notice and documented use of the official calculator is essential.  
Refusing an Unlawful Increase Isn’t Illegal 
A tenant rejecting an increase outside the RERA framework can’t be evicted solely for that refusal.  
Eviction Must Be for Legitimate Reasons 
Only statutory causes (sale, personal use, reno, demolition, or serious breach) support eviction with 
the required notice.  
 
Dispute Resolution Is Structured 
Disputes over increases, eviction notices, or other tenancy issues go through the Rental Dispute 
Settlement Centre, which enforces RERA law.
